🔄 1. Overdraft Fee

High RiskCommon in: Checking AccountsAvg Cost: $30–$40 per transaction

What It Is

An overdraft fee is a charge incurred when a transaction exceeds the available balance in a checking account. The bank covers the shortfall as a courtesy and charges a flat fee per transaction. Some banks also charge an "extended overdraft fee" if the account remains negative for multiple days.

Why Banks Charge It

Overdraft fees generate over $15 billion annually for U.S. banks. They are a core revenue stream, not a cost-recovery mechanism. Banks design transaction processing systems — including transaction reordering — to maximize the number of overdraft fees a customer incurs. Processing larger transactions first depletes the balance faster, triggering multiple fees from smaller subsequent transactions.

When It Is Legitimate

When a consumer explicitly opts into overdraft protection and uses it as a short-term liquidity tool, a reasonable overdraft fee (typically $25–$35) may be considered legitimate for the service provided. Some banks offer overdraft lines of credit with lower effective costs than flat fees.

When It May Be Excessive

When combined with transaction reordering practices that deliberately maximize fee volume. A single day of small purchases can trigger 5–10 overdraft fees if transactions are processed largest-first. Fees exceeding $35 per transaction, extended overdraft fees on top of standard fees, or fees triggered by holds rather than actual debits may be excessive.

Warning Signs

Multiple overdraft fees in a single day; transactions posting in a different order than they occurred; fees on debit card authorizations that later settled for less; accounts with overdraft protection still incurring fees; extended overdraft fees added on top of standard fees.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is your transaction processing order — largest to smallest, or chronological?" "Can you waive this overdraft fee as a courtesy?" "Do you offer a cheaper overdraft line of credit?" "How many overdraft fees can I incur in one day?" "Can I opt out of overdraft coverage for debit card transactions?"

Documents Where It Appears

Bank statements, monthly account summaries, electronic transaction histories, online banking portals, account terms and conditions disclosures.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan bank statements to identify overdraft fee patterns, flag transaction reordering, calculate total annual fee exposure, and compare fee frequency against account activity to identify potential systemic fee optimization by the bank.

❌ 2. NSF (Non-Sufficient Funds) Fee

High RiskCommon in: Checking AccountsAvg Cost: $30–$40 per occurrence

What It Is

An NSF fee is charged when a transaction is presented against an account with insufficient funds and the bank declines to process it. Unlike an overdraft fee (which covers the transaction), an NSF fee is charged for rejecting the transaction.

Why Banks Charge It

NSF fees serve as both revenue and a deterrent. However, critics argue they disproportionately affect lower-income consumers and can cascade — one bounced payment can trigger NSF fees from the originating bank and returned-check fees from the recipient.

When It Is Legitimate

A single NSF fee for an exceptional insufficient-funds event may be reasonable to cover the bank's processing costs and as a deterrent against writing checks without sufficient funds.

When It May Be Excessive

When combined with overdraft fees on the same transaction; when charged on micro-transactions under $5; when multiple NSF fees arise from a single merchant re-submitting the same payment multiple times.

Warning Signs

Both NSF and overdraft fees appearing for the same transaction; multiple fees from a single merchant attempt; fees on accounts with overdraft protection enabled.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Can you waive this NSF fee?" "Why was this transaction charged both an NSF fee and an overdraft fee?" "What is your policy on re-presented items?"

Documents Where It Appears

Bank statements, account transaction histories, monthly fee summaries.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can detect patterns where NSF fees and overdraft fees overlap on the same transactions, flag excessive fee cascades, and calculate the total cost of insufficient-fund events.

🏧 3. ATM Surcharge Fee

Medium RiskCommon in: Out-of-Network ATMsAvg Cost: $2–$5 per transaction

What It Is

A fee charged for using an ATM outside of your bank's network. This can include a surcharge from the ATM owner and an additional fee from your own bank for using a non-network machine.

Why Banks Charge It

ATM network fees generate significant revenue. Out-of-network ATMs charge for the convenience of cash access, and banks charge to discourage expensive out-of-network usage. However, the combined fees can make small cash withdrawals disproportionately expensive.

When It Is Legitimate

A modest fee ($2–$3) for using an out-of-network ATM where the convenience of cash access has value and the fee is clearly disclosed before the transaction.

When It May Be Excessive

Combined fees exceeding $5 per transaction; fees on ATMs that do not display the surcharge amount before processing; banks charging out-of-network fees plus ATM-owner surcharges without clear disclosure.

Warning Signs

Unexpected fees on bank statements for ATM withdrawals; fees on transactions where no fee was displayed; multiple fees for a single withdrawal.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Does my bank reimburse out-of-network ATM fees?" "What are my bank's partner ATM networks?" "Does my account tier include fee-free ATM usage?"

Documents Where It Appears

Bank statements, electronic transaction logs, monthly fee summaries, online banking transaction details.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan bank statements to calculate total ATM fee expenditure, identify patterns of out-of-network usage, and recommend account types or banking relationships that reduce or eliminate these fees.

📅 4. Monthly Maintenance Fee

Medium RiskCommon in: Checking & Savings AccountsAvg Cost: $5–$25 per month

What It Is

A recurring monthly fee charged for maintaining a checking or savings account. Many banks waive this fee if the account holder maintains a minimum balance, sets up direct deposit, or meets other conditions.

Why Banks Charge It

Maintenance fees generate predictable recurring revenue and incentivize customers to maintain higher balances. The fees are often hidden in account disclosures rather than clearly communicated at account opening.

When It Is Legitimate

When clearly disclosed at account opening and easily avoided by meeting straightforward waiver conditions (e.g., $500 minimum balance or one direct deposit per month).

When It May Be Excessive

When waiver conditions are deliberately difficult to meet; when fees are charged despite the customer meeting waiver conditions; when multiple maintenance fees appear on linked accounts (checking + savings).

Warning Signs

Recurring monthly deductions that reduce balance over time; fees appearing even though you believe conditions are met; different fee amounts each month.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"How can I have this monthly fee waived?" "Does my account type include fee-free banking?" "Can you switch me to an account without monthly fees?"

Documents Where It Appears

Monthly bank statements, account fee schedules, account opening disclosures, online banking transaction history.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan 12 months of bank statements to calculate total maintenance fee expenditure, identify months where fees were improperly charged despite meeting waiver conditions, and recommend fee-free account alternatives.

🔀 5. Transaction Reordering (Overdraft Sequencing) Fee

High RiskCommon in: Checking AccountsAvg Cost: Triggers multiple overdraft fees

What It Is

A practice where banks post transactions in high-to-low dollar amount order rather than chronological order, maximizing the number of overdraft fees a customer incurs by depleting the balance faster with larger transactions first.

Why Banks Charge It

Transaction reordering is a deliberate fee-maximization strategy. By processing a $100 transaction before five $10 transactions when the account has $90, the $100 transaction triggers one overdraft, and the five $10 transactions trigger five more.

When It Is Legitimate

This practice is widely considered predatory by consumer advocacy groups including the CFPB. There is no legitimate consumer benefit to large-first transaction processing.

When It May Be Excessive

Always excessive when used to maximize fee revenue. The practice has been the subject of multiple regulatory actions and class-action lawsuits.

Warning Signs

Multiple overdraft fees on days when transactions were all relatively small; transactions posting in a different order than they occurred; fees appearing on debit card transactions that authorized at a higher amount and settled lower.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Does your bank process transactions largest-to-smallest or chronologically?" "Can you switch my account to chronological processing?" "Will you refund overdraft fees caused by transaction reordering?"

Documents Where It Appears

Detailed transaction histories, account statements showing posting order, bank fee schedules and transaction processing disclosures.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can analyze account transaction history to identify reordering patterns, calculate how much was paid in fees due to non-chronological processing, and generate evidence for fee refund requests.

📄 6. Loan Origination Fee

Medium RiskCommon in: Mortgages, Personal Loans, Auto LoansAvg Cost: 0.5%–2% of loan amount

What It Is

A fee charged by a lender for processing a new loan application. It covers underwriting, documentation, and administrative costs. On a $300,000 mortgage, a 1% origination fee equals $3,000.

Why Lenders Charge It

Lenders present origination fees as covering the cost of evaluating and processing loan applications. However, these fees are often padded above actual processing costs and represent significant profit centers.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee is clearly disclosed in the Loan Estimate, represents a reasonable percentage of the loan amount (0.5–1%), and is competitive with other lenders offering similar terms.

When It May Be Excessive

When origination fees exceed 2% without corresponding rate benefits; when fees appear on top of separate application, processing, or underwriting fees; when the fee is not clearly disclosed in the initial loan estimate.

Warning Signs

Multiple loan processing fees listed separately; origination fee plus separate underwriting fee; origination fee exceeding 2% without explanation; fee not appearing on the initial Loan Estimate.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Is this origination fee negotiable?" "Can I get a lender credit to offset the origination fee in exchange for a slightly higher rate?" "Are there any other processing fees beyond the origination fee?"

Documents Where It Appears

Loan Estimate (section A), Closing Disclosure, promissory notes, loan agreements.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can compare your Loan Estimate against market averages for your loan type and credit profile, flag origination fees above typical ranges, and identify duplicate or overlapping processing fees.

🔒 7. Prepayment Penalty

High RiskCommon in: Mortgages, Personal LoansAvg Cost: 2%–5% of remaining balance

What It Is

A fee charged for paying off a loan before the scheduled term ends. This penalizes borrowers for making extra payments, refinancing, or selling a home before the loan matures.

Why Lenders Charge It

Lenders expect a certain return from interest over the loan term. Early payoff reduces their profit, so prepayment penalties compensate for lost interest revenue.

When It Is Legitimate

Prepayment penalties are legitimate when clearly disclosed in the loan agreement and limited to the first 2–3 years of the loan term. Some loans may offer a lower rate in exchange for including a prepayment penalty.

When It May Be Excessive

When the penalty extends beyond 3 years; when the penalty amount exceeds 2% of the outstanding balance; when applied to a loan where the borrower did not receive a clearly disclosed rate benefit in exchange.

Warning Signs

Loan documents mentioning "prepayment" or "early payoff penalties" in the fine print; lenders discouraging extra payments; loan agreements with complex prepayment calculation formulas.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Does this loan have a prepayment penalty?" "How much would the penalty be if I pay off the loan early?" "How long does the prepayment penalty period last?" "Is there a less expensive loan option without a prepayment penalty?"

Documents Where It Appears

Promissory notes, loan agreements, Truth in Lending disclosures, Closing Disclosure (if real estate).

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan loan documents for prepayment penalty clauses, calculate the exact penalty amount under different payoff scenarios, and flag terms that may be excessive based on loan type and jurisdiction.

💸 8. Wire Transfer Fee

StandardCommon in: All Bank AccountsAvg Cost: $15–$50 per transfer

What It Is

A fee charged for sending or receiving a wire transfer — an electronic transfer of funds from one bank to another. Domestic wires cost less than international wires.

Why Banks Charge It

Wire transfers require manual processing, security verification, and access to payment networks. The fee covers these operational costs.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee is clearly disclosed before the transfer is initiated, competitive with other banks, and the customer is offered alternative transfer methods (ACH, Zelle) at lower or no cost.

When It May Be Excessive

When both sending and receiving banks charge fees on the same transaction without disclosure; when fees for electronic transfers exceed $35 domestic or $60 international; when fee is not disclosed before authorization.

Warning Signs

Unexpected fees on bank statements after completing a wire transfer; wire fee disclosures buried in account terms rather than shown at transaction time.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is the wire transfer fee, and is there a less expensive alternative?" "Does my account type include free wire transfers?" "Are there both sending and receiving fees?"

Documents Where It Appears

Bank statements, transaction receipts, account fee schedules.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan bank statements to identify wire transfer fee patterns and calculate total annual wire-related costs, helping consumers decide if alternative transfer methods would be more cost-effective.

🌍 9. Foreign Transaction Fee

Medium RiskCommon in: Credit Cards, Debit CardsAvg Cost: 1%–3% of transaction

What It Is

A fee charged on credit or debit card transactions that process in a foreign currency or through a foreign bank. It typically ranges from 1% to 3% of the transaction amount.

Why Banks Charge It

Banks cite currency conversion costs and network processing fees. However, the actual cost of currency conversion is typically less than 0.5%, making the 3% fee a significant profit source.

When It Is Legitimate

When clearly disclosed in cardholder terms, reasonable (1–2%), and the card offers benefits like travel insurance or rewards that offset the cost.

When It May Be Excessive

When the fee exceeds 3%; when applied to transactions in U.S. dollars processed through foreign intermediaries; when the fee is not disclosed in card terms.

Warning Signs

Credit card statements showing "foreign transaction fee" on purchases made while traveling; fees on online purchases from foreign merchants.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Does my credit card charge foreign transaction fees?" "What is the exact percentage?" "Is there a card option without foreign transaction fees?"

Documents Where It Appears

Credit card statements, cardholder agreements, transaction receipts.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan credit card statements to identify all foreign transaction fees, calculate total annual cost, and recommend card alternatives that eliminate these fees.

💵 10. Cash Advance Fee

High RiskCommon in: Credit CardsAvg Cost: 3%–5% of advance + immediate interest

What It Is

A fee charged when using a credit card to obtain cash — either through an ATM, bank withdrawal, or convenience check. Unlike purchases, cash advances typically have no grace period and begin accruing interest immediately at a higher APR.

Why Banks Charge It

Cash advances are higher risk for banks (no grace period, possibility of non-repayment), and the combination of upfront fees plus immediate interest at a higher rate makes them highly profitable.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee and interest terms are clearly disclosed before the transaction, and the consumer uses it for genuine emergency cash needs.

When It May Be Excessive

When fees exceed 5% plus immediate interest at rates above 25% APR; when consumers are not clearly warned that cash advances have different terms than purchases; when convenience checks are mailed unsolicited with misleading terms.

Warning Signs

Credit card terms showing different APRs for purchases vs. cash advances; unsolicited convenience checks in the mail; ATM screens offering cash advances without highlighting the fee and interest structure.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is the cash advance APR versus my purchase APR?" "Is there a grace period for cash advances?" "What is the cash advance fee percentage and minimum?"

Documents Where It Appears

Credit card agreements, monthly statements showing cash advance transactions, Schumer Box disclosures.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan credit card statements to identify cash advance transactions and calculate the true cost including fees and interest, comparing it against alternative funding sources.
