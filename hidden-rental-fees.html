📋 1. Rental Application Fee

Medium RiskOn: Apartment RentalsAvg: $30–$75 per application

What It Is

A non-refundable fee charged to process a rental application, typically covering credit checks, background checks, and administrative processing of the application.

Why Companies Charge It

Landlords and property managers claim fees cover screening costs. However, the actual cost of a credit and background check is typically $15–$30, making fee amounts above that range potential profit centers.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee is reasonable ($25–$50), covers actual screening costs, and is consistent across all applicants.

When It May Be Excessive

When fees exceed $75 per applicant; when fees are charged for applications that are not processed; when multiple application fees are required for the same rental unit.

Warning Signs

Application fees that seem high compared to the rental market average; fees described as "non-refundable" without a breakdown of what they cover.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is the application fee and what does it cover?" "Is the fee refundable if my application is not processed?"

Documents Where It Appears

Rental applications, lease agreements, fee schedules, receipts.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan rental applications and fee schedules to identify application fees, compare them against local averages, and flag excessive charges.

🏢 2. Administrative Fee (Rental)

Medium RiskOn: Apartment RentalsAvg: $100–$500/year

What It Is

A recurring fee charged by landlords for administrative costs such as lease processing, record keeping, and property management overhead. Some landlords charge this as a monthly or annual add-on.

Why Companies Charge It

Property management companies add administrative fees to generate additional revenue beyond the base rent. These fees are often not negotiable and may not be clearly disclosed.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee is clearly disclosed before lease signing, is reasonable, and covers specific administrative services.

When It May Be Excessive

When the admin fee exceeds 5% of monthly rent; when multiple administrative fees appear; when the fee is not mentioned until lease signing.

Warning Signs

"Administrative fee" or "management fee" listed separately from base rent; fees that increase annually without clear justification.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Are there any monthly fees beyond the base rent?" "What services does the administrative fee cover?"

Documents Where It Appears

Lease agreements, rental fee schedules, monthly billing statements.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan lease agreements and fee schedules to identify administrative fees, calculate the effective monthly cost, and flag excessive charges relative to market norms.

🐾 3. Pet Rent / Pet Deposit

Medium RiskOn: Pet-Friendly RentalsAvg: $25–$75/month + $200–$500 deposit

What It Is

A monthly fee or one-time deposit charged for having a pet in a rental unit. Some landlords charge both a non-refundable pet fee and monthly pet rent, significantly increasing the effective rent.

Why Companies Charge It

Pet fees cover potential damage and cleaning costs. However, non-refundable fees plus monthly rent can substantially exceed any actual damage a pet might cause.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee is clearly disclosed, reasonable ($25–$50/month per pet), and the pet policy is applied consistently.

When It May Be Excessive

When combined non-refundable fees and pet rent exceed $100/month per pet; when deposits are charged on top of non-refundable fees without justification.

Warning Signs

Pet fees that are charged per pet and per month; non-refundable deposits equal to or exceeding refundable deposits.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is the one-time pet fee versus monthly pet rent?" "Is the pet deposit refundable?"

Documents Where It Appears

Lease agreements, pet addendums, fee schedules.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan lease documents to identify all pet-related charges, calculate total pet costs, and compare against typical pet fee structures in the area.

🔑 4. Move-Out Fee / Cleaning Fee

Medium RiskOn: Apartment RentalsAvg: $100–$500+

What It Is

A fee charged at move-out for cleaning, repairs, or painting of the rental unit. Some landlords charge a flat move-out fee regardless of the actual condition of the unit.

Why Companies Charge It

Move-out fees are intended to cover turnover costs between tenants. However, flat fees charged regardless of unit condition may result in tenants paying for cleaning they don't need.

When It Is Legitimate

When cleaning fees are based on actual condition and are itemized with receipts upon request.

When It May Be Excessive

When a flat move-out fee is charged regardless of unit condition; when cleaning fees are charged on top of a non-refundable cleaning deposit; when fees exceed actual documented costs.

Warning Signs

Leases that specify a "mandatory move-out cleaning fee" regardless of condition requirements for professional cleaning with receipt verification.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Is there a mandatory move-out fee?" "Can I clean the unit myself to avoid a cleaning charge?" "Will you provide receipts for any deductions from my deposit?"

Documents Where It Appears

Lease agreements, move-out checklists, security deposit disposition statements.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan lease agreements to identify mandatory move-out fees, security deposit deduction policies, and flag leases where flat fees may be excessive.

✂️ 5. Lease Break / Early Termination Fee

High RiskOn: Apartment RentalsAvg: 1–3 months' rent

What It Is

A penalty for ending a lease before the end of its term. Lease break fees can range from one month's rent to the full remaining balance of the lease.

Why Companies Charge It

Landlords use lease break fees to mitigate the financial impact of a vacant unit. However, some fee structures far exceed the landlord's actual loss.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee is limited to 1–2 months' rent, clearly disclosed in the lease, and the landlord is required to mitigate damages by attempting to re-rent the unit.

When It May Be Excessive

When the fee exceeds 2 months' rent; when the landlord does not attempt to re-rent the unit; when the fee is charged in addition to the remaining lease balance.

Warning Signs

Lease break fees described as "full remaining balance" language that waives the landlord's duty to mitigate damages.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is the lease break fee?" "Is the landlord required to re-rent the unit?" "Are there any circumstances where the fee is waived?"

Documents Where It Appears

Lease agreements, early termination addendums, rental policies.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan lease agreements to identify early termination clauses, calculate the maximum penalty, and flag terms that may be unreasonable under state law.
