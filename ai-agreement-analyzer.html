Common Hidden Fees Found in Agreements

Agreements hide fees in many ways. Some are obvious line items buried on page eight. Others are disguised as standard provisions with hidden cost implications. An AI agreement analyzer catches both types.

Administrative fees appear in service agreements, lease contracts, and subscription terms. They are often described as "processing fees," "account maintenance charges," or "service and handling." The amounts may seem small individually, but they add up over the term of the agreement.

Markup clauses allow the provider to add a percentage to materials, labor, or subcontractor costs without your explicit approval for each transaction. These clauses are common in contractor agreements and managed service contracts. A markup clause might say "materials at cost plus 15 percent" buried inside a paragraph about quality standards.

Change order provisions let the other party adjust pricing when scope changes occur. The key detail is whether the pricing adjustment is defined or open-ended. Agreements with undefined change order pricing are effectively asking you to sign a blank check for any future modifications.

Automatic renewal clauses renew your agreement without notice. Many include price increases that take effect at renewal without requiring your explicit consent. A three-year subscription at $29 per month can become $39 per month at renewal, and you might not notice for months.

Early termination penalties vary widely. Some are reasonable and cover actual costs. Others are punitive and designed to lock you into the agreement regardless of changes in your circumstances. The AI identifies these penalties and compares them against typical ranges for your type of agreement.

Late payment structures can include interest rates that far exceed what is reasonable. Some agreements charge flat fees for late payments that are disproportionate to the administrative cost of processing a late payment. Others use compounding interest that can turn a small oversight into a significant charge.

The AI identifies all of these fee types regardless of what terminology the agreement uses. This is important because drafters often use creative language to describe fees. Instead of "late fee," an agreement might say "administrative adjustment for payment recalculations." The AI recognizes these descriptions by their structure and context.

Understanding what fees exist in an agreement before signing gives you the opportunity to negotiate. Some fees are negotiable. Others are standard industry practice. Knowing the difference helps you decide which terms to challenge and which to accept.
