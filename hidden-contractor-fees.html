🔥 1. Emergency Labor Surcharge

High RiskCommon in: HVAC, Plumbing, ElectricalAvg: $85/hr standard → $225/hr+ emergency

What It Is

An elevated hourly rate charged for emergency or after-hours service calls. While some premium for urgent service is reasonable, emergency surcharges in the home services industry are frequently inflated to 2–3 times the standard rate, with limited correlation to actual after-hours costs.

Why Contractors Charge It

Contractors cite overtime pay for staff, opportunity cost of pulling technicians from scheduled work, and the inconvenience of after-hours service. However, many residential service companies use emergency pricing as a primary profit center.

When It Is Legitimate

A modest premium — typically 25–50% above standard rates — for genuine after-hours service when the contractor can demonstrate actual overtime costs and the customer was informed of the rate before service began.

When It May Be Excessive

When the emergency rate exceeds $200/hour; when the premium is more than 100% of the standard rate; when the emergency rate is applied to calls that are not true emergencies (routine issues that could wait); when the rate is not disclosed before service begins.

Warning Signs

Hourly rates quoted as "emergency" or "after-hours" without specific amounts; estimates that switch from standard to emergency pricing without explanation; invoices showing a higher rate than what was discussed on the phone.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is your standard hourly rate versus your emergency rate?" "Can you put the emergency rate in writing before you start work?" "What specifically qualifies as an emergency call?" "Is this a true emergency or can it wait until regular business hours?"

Documents Where It Appears

Service invoices, work orders, contractor estimates, service agreements.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan contractor estimates and invoices to identify emergency surcharges, calculate the premium as a percentage of standard rates, compare against industry benchmarks for your region, and flag rates exceeding typical ranges.

🛠️ 2. Material Markup Fee

Medium RiskCommon in: All Construction TradesTypical: 10–15% vs Excessive: 30–50%+

What It Is

A markup on the cost of materials used in a construction project. Contractors typically charge more for materials than they pay their suppliers. The markup covers procurement, storage, handling, and warranty risk.

Why Contractors Charge It

A reasonable markup is standard practice to cover material procurement costs. However, markups of 30–50% or more are common in some trades, significantly exceeding the actual cost of procurement.

When It Is Legitimate

A material markup of 10–15% is standard and reasonable to cover procurement, storage, and handling. Some contractors disclose the markup explicitly in their estimate.

When It May Be Excessive

When markups exceed 25% without disclosure; when the contractor charges full retail price for materials they can purchase at wholesale and adds a markup on top; when material pricing is not itemized, preventing comparison shopping.

Warning Signs

Material costs that appear significantly higher than retail; estimates that lump materials and labor together without itemization; contractors who refuse to provide receipts for materials.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Can I see the invoices for materials?" "What markup do you charge on materials?" "Would you accept materials I purchase myself?" "Can you provide an itemized breakdown of material costs?"

Documents Where It Appears

Contractor estimates, invoices, proposals, material lists.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can compare material costs in your estimate against retail and wholesale prices, calculate the effective markup percentage, and flag material costs that significantly exceed market rates for the same items in your area.

📝 3. Change Order Fee

High RiskCommon in: Renovation, ConstructionAvg Impact: 10–30% of original contract

What It Is

A charge for modifying the original scope of work in a construction contract. Change orders can include fees for the change itself (administration), plus higher rates for the modified work, and markups on any new materials required.

Why Contractors Charge It

Change orders disrupt scheduling and may require new materials or subcontractors. However, some contractors use low initial bids followed by profitable change orders as a deliberate business model.

When It Is Legitimate

When the change is genuinely outside the original scope, the pricing is clearly disclosed before work begins, and the change order fee is limited to a reasonable administrative charge (typically $100–$300 or a stated percentage).

When It May Be Excessive

When change order rates are higher than the rates in the original contract; when the original contract is deliberately vague, forcing numerous change orders; when change order fees exceed 15–20% of the original contract value.

Warning Signs

Contracts with vague scope descriptions; extremely low initial bids that seem too good to be true; contracts that don't specify change order pricing in advance.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"How do you price change orders?" "Are change order rates the same as the original contract rates?" "How many change orders do your typical projects involve?" "Can you provide an allowance for potential changes in the original estimate?"

Documents Where It Appears

Construction contracts, change order forms, final invoices, project proposals.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan the original contract and change order documentation to identify rate differences between original work and change orders, flag pricing patterns that suggest lowball bidding, and calculate the total impact of change orders on the original budget.

🧩 4. Subcontractor Layering Fee

Medium RiskCommon in: Renovation, General ContractingAvg Impact: 10–25% addition to sub costs

What It Is

A markup added by a general contractor on top of subcontractor pricing. When a GC hires an electrician, plumber, or roofer, they may add a fee for managing the subcontractor — often 10–25% of the subcontractor's charge.

Why Contractors Charge It

The GC bears the risk of subcontractor performance and manages scheduling, quality control, and payment. A reasonable fee for this service is standard industry practice.

When It Is Legitimate

A disclosed subcontractor management fee of 10–15% is reasonable, particularly on complex projects with multiple trades. The percentage should be clearly stated in the contract.

When It May Be Excessive

When the layering fee exceeds 20%; when the GC charges markups on both materials and subcontractor fees without disclosure; when the fee is not mentioned in the original contract but appears on invoices.

Warning Signs

Subcontractor line items on invoices that are higher than competitive bids; contracts that reference sub fees without stating the markup percentage; line items labeled "supervision" or "management" on top of subcontractor charges.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What percentage do you add to subcontractor fees?" "Can I see the actual subcontractor quotes?" "Is your management fee included in the subcontractor line items or listed separately?"

Documents Where It Appears

Construction contracts, project estimates, invoices, subcontractor quotes.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can analyze contract terms to identify subcontractor layering language, compare quoted subcontractor amounts against typical market rates, and flag total management fees that exceed reasonable percentages.

📋 5. Permit Fee Padding

Medium RiskCommon in: Renovation, New ConstructionAvg Impact: $100–$500+ added

What It Is

Charging the homeowner more than the actual cost of building permits. A permit that costs the contractor $100 may appear as $250 on the homeowner's estimate — the $150 difference is pure profit.

Why Contractors Charge It

Some contractors add a markup to permits as a convenience fee or to cover the time spent pulling permits. Others pad permits as an easy way to increase the total estimate without scrutiny.

When It Is Legitimate

When the contractor discloses the actual permit cost and adds a reasonable administrative fee ($25–$50) for the time spent obtaining permits.

When It May Be Excessive

When the permit charge is significantly higher than the municipality's published fee schedule; when the contractor refuses to provide a copy of the paid permit receipt.

Warning Signs

Permit fees that seem high for the type of work; contractors who are vague about permit costs; line items labeled "permit fees" with no breakdown.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is the actual cost of the building permit?" "Can you show me the permit receipt after it's pulled?" "What city department issues this permit and what are their published fees?"

Documents Where It Appears

Contractor estimates, invoices, permit applications, municipal fee schedules.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can compare permit charges in your estimate against typical municipal fee schedules for your area and project type, flag permit charges that appear padded, and provide documentation for disputing excessive permit fees.

❄️ 6. Refrigerant Overpricing (HVAC)

High RiskCommon in: HVAC ServiceAvg Impact: $200–$800+ per pound

What It Is

Charging excessively high prices for refrigerant during HVAC service calls. Refrigerant that costs contractors $50–$150 per pound (depending on type) is frequently billed to consumers at $200–$800+ per pound — a markup of 200–500% or more.

Why Contractors Charge It

Refrigerant pricing is opaque, and consumers rarely know the wholesale cost. Some HVAC companies use refrigerant markup as a significant profit center, particularly for emergency service calls.

When It Is Legitimate

A reasonable markup (50–100% over wholesale) to cover inventory costs, handling, and the refrigerant itself is standard. The charge should be clearly listed per pound.

When It May Be Excessive

When the price per pound exceeds $200 for common refrigerants (like R-410A); when the total refrigerant charge is not broken down by pound; when refrigerant pricing is not disclosed before service.

Warning Signs

Invoices with a single refrigerant line item but no per-pound price; refrigerant charges that seem disproportionate to the amount needed; contractors who refuse to quote refrigerant pricing upfront.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is your price per pound for refrigerant?" "How many pounds of refrigerant do I need?" "Can you provide a written estimate that includes the per-pound cost?"

Documents Where It Appears

HVAC service invoices, repair estimates, work orders.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan HVAC invoices to identify refrigerant charges, calculate the per-pound cost, compare against current wholesale and retail refrigerant prices, and flag markups exceeding reasonable thresholds.

🔧 7. Diagnostic / Trip Fee

StandardCommon in: Plumbing, Electrical, HVACAvg: $50–$150

What It Is

A flat fee charged just for a service professional to arrive and diagnose a problem. This fee may be applied to the repair cost if work is performed, or charged separately if the customer declines the repair.

Why Contractors Charge It

The diagnostic/trip fee covers the cost of sending a technician to your location, including travel time, vehicle costs, and the technician's time for the initial evaluation.

When It Is Legitimate

When the fee is clearly disclosed before the technician arrives, is reasonable for the area ($50–$100 typically), and is credited toward the repair if work is performed.

When It May Be Excessive

When the fee exceeds $150; when the fee is not disclosed before scheduling; when the fee is charged in addition to an hourly rate that already includes travel time; when the fee is not credited toward repairs.

Warning Signs

Companies that won't quote a trip fee over the phone; fees described as "non-refundable" or "service charge" without explanation.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"What is your diagnostic or trip fee?" "Is the trip fee applied to the repair cost if I proceed?" "Is the trip fee refundable if you can't fix the problem?"

Documents Where It Appears

Service invoices, work orders, estimates, service agreements.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can scan invoices to identify trip or diagnostic fees, verify whether the fee was credited toward the final repair cost, and compare fee amounts against industry averages for your service type and region.

📐 8. Material Allowance Bait & Switch

High RiskCommon in: Renovation, Flooring, CabinetryAvg Impact: $1,000–$5,000+

What It Is

A practice where a contractor provides an allowance for materials (e.g., "$5,000 allowance for flooring") but the materials the homeowner wants cost significantly more. The contractor pockets the difference between the allowance and the actual cost, or uses low-quality materials to stay within the allowance.

Why Contractors Charge It

Low allowances make the initial estimate look more competitive. Contractors know most homeowners will select options that exceed the allowance, generating additional profit through the markup on the difference.

When It Is Legitimate

When allowances are set at realistic levels based on the homeowner's stated preferences, and the contractor provides receipts for all material purchases with any allowance surplus refunded to the homeowner.

When It May Be Excessive

When allowances are intentionally set below market rates for the specified materials; when the contractor keeps unused allowance amounts; when changes to materials require a change order with higher labor rates.

Warning Signs

Allowances that seem low for the quality of materials discussed; contracts that don't specify what happens to unused allowance amounts; contractors who discourage selecting materials within the allowance.

Consumer Questions to Ask

"Is the material allowance based on actual prices I've seen?" "What happens if I select materials that cost less than the allowance?" "What happens if materials cost more than the allowance?" "Can I purchase materials myself to avoid the allowance issue?"

Documents Where It Appears

Construction contracts, allowance schedules, change orders, material selection worksheets.

How AI Analysis Can Help

AI can analyze construction contracts to identify allowance language, compare allowance amounts against market prices for specified materials, and flag allowances that are likely to result in cost overruns.
